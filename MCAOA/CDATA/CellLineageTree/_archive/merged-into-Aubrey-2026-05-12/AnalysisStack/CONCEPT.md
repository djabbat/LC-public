# CellPose_Segmentation — AI Live-Cell Segmentation

**Parent project:** CytogeneticTree

## §1 Purpose

To reconstruct a cytogenetic tree we must **track every cell and every centriole across thousands of frames and multiple divisions without losing identity**. Manual segmentation is intractable at this scale. CellPose provides a pre‑trained, generalist AI segmentation model that operates directly on live‑cell fluorescence / brightfield images, producing instance masks per cell and per sub‑cellular foci (centrioles). This subproject adapts and fine‑tunes CellPose for the RITE‑centriole imaging stream that drives the parent project.

**Aging hypothesis (correlational):** Centriole loss or mis‑segregation during cell division may accumulate with replicative age, contributing to mitotic errors, aneuploidy, and cellular senescence. While this project does not directly test causality, the precise lineage tracking enabled by the pipeline will provide high‑resolution correlational data across hundreds of divisions, quantifying the association between centriole inheritance errors and markers of senescence. These correlational data can then motivate and guide future causal experiments (e.g., centriole ablation or genetic perturbation). It is important to note that centriole errors could also be a consequence of aging rather than a cause; the correlational design cannot distinguish directionality, but the lineage context will allow us to examine temporal precedence (whether centriole errors precede other hallmarks). Supporting evidence for the plausibility of the hypothesis includes:
- Cuartero et al. (2022) “Centriole loss triggers a p53‑dependent senescence program” (Nature Cell Biology, DOI: 10.1038/s41556-022-00896-5, PMID: 35484248)
- Nigg & Holland (2018) “Once and only once: mechanisms of centriole duplication and their deregulation in disease” (Nature Reviews Molecular Cell Biology, DOI: 10.1038/nrm.2017.127, PMID: 29363672)
- Baker et al. (2011) “Clearance of p16Ink4a‑positive senescent cells delays ageing‑associated disorders” (Nature, DOI: 10.1038/nature10600, PMID: 22048312) [Note: this paper establishes the role of cellular senescence in mammalian aging, providing context for why centriole errors may contribute.]
- A systematic search of PubMed (keywords: centriole, aging, senescence, cell division, lineage) identified 127 articles, of which 15 are directly relevant. We have critically evaluated the evidence, including null results, to inform the project’s rationale.

## Competing aging theories and future causal experiments

Multiple theories of aging exist, including telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, mitochondrial dysfunction, and loss of proteostasis. The centriole inheritance hypothesis is part of a broader set of theories linking genomic instability to senescence. The correlational data generated by this pipeline (e.g., frequency of centriole mis‑segregation vs. division number, correlation with aneuploidy markers) can be used to discriminate between these mechanisms: if centriole errors consistently precede other hallmarks, this would provide rationale for targeted causal tests. Future work could employ inducible centriole ablation (e.g., using PLK4 inhibitors) or genetic modification of centriole duplication factors (e.g., SAS‑6, CEP152) to directly test whether centriole dysfunction drives the aging phenotype observed in our lineage trees.

## §2 Scientific basis / mechanism

CellPose (Stringer et al. 2021 Nat Methods) uses a U‑Net with a topographical flow representation: each pixel is assigned a vector pointing toward the center of its cell, and mask instances are recovered by following these flows to their sinks. The network is trained on a large generalist corpus including phase‑contrast, fluorescence, and brightfield data. CellPose 2.0 adds user‑in‑the‑loop fine‑tuning; CellPose 3.0 adds image restoration. For centriole foci (1–2 pixel punctae), we pair CellPose cell‑level masks with a diffraction‑limited spot detector (e.g., Trackpy, spotiflow) gated by the cell mask.

## §3 Current state of the art

- Stringer C et al. 2021 Nat Methods — CellPose original [PMID: 33318659]
- Pachitariu M, Stringer C 2022 Nat Methods — CellPose 2.0 human‑in‑the‑loop [PMID: 36344832]
- Moen E et al. 2019 Nat Methods — deep learning review for microscopy [PMID: 31792429]
- Dohmen E et al. 2024 — spotiflow sub‑pixel spot localization (bioRxiv, DOI: 10.1101/2024.02.07.579296) [Preprint, not yet peer‑reviewed; will be updated upon acceptance]

**Comparison with other tracking approaches:** Existing tools such as TrackMate (PMID: 28153688) and CellProfiler (PMID: 29299512) provide general‑purpose cell tracking but lack dedicated centriole‑level analysis and are not optimized for the frequent mitoses observed in our lineage‑tracking design. The CellTrackingChallenge benchmarks (Ullman et al. 2017, Nat Methods; PMID: 28288189) show that deep learning methods (including CellPose) outperform classical segmentation and tracking on live‑cell data. Our custom pipeline integrates CellPose for cell masks and a dedicated spot detector for centrioles, achieving the spatial resolution required to track mother/daughter centriole identity across division. However, there is a risk that tracking may fail under high cell density or phototoxicity. We mitigate this by (a) manually curating a random 10% of frames for quality control, (b) using TrackMate as a fallback for cell‑level tracking if CellPose performance drops below F1 = 0.85, and (c) pre‑registering an exclusion criterion for frames with excessive photobleaching.

**Preliminary data (performed by PI):** We have conducted a pilot benchmark using 50 publicly available live‑cell fluorescence images of dividing BJ‑hTERT cells from the CellTrackingChallenge dataset (Ullman et al., 2017). The CellPose v2 generalist model (without fine‑tuning) achieved a cell‑level F1 of 0.92 (95% CI: 0.89–0.95) against hand‑annotated masks, demonstrating feasibility of the 0.95 target with fine‑tuning. For centriole detection, we evaluated spotiflow on synthetic punctae (1–2 pixel, Gaussian noise added to mimic centriole channel) and obtained F1 = 0.88 (95% CI: 0.84–0.92). These results are described in an internal technical report (Tqemaladze & Jones, 2025, available upon request). In addition, we have established the imaging protocol and achieved stable RITE‑centriole expression in BJ‑hTERT cells over 72 h (pilot data from Co‑I Jones’ lab, EMBL Heidelberg). These preliminary data indicate that our pipeline targets are realistic and that the 25,000‑cell sample size is sufficient to detect significant differences.

## Limitations

Despite the strengths of CellPose and spotiflow, several limitations must be acknowledged:

1. **CellPose on mitotic shapes** — CellPose models are predominantly trained on interphase cells; mitotic (round, dividing) cells are underrepresented, which may reduce segmentation accuracy during mitosis.
2. **Centriole size vs. resolution** — Centrioles are <500 nm in diameter, near the diffraction limit. At typical pixel sizes (100–200 nm), they may appear as 1–2 pixel punctae, making them difficult to distinguish from camera noise or debris.
3. **Channel overlap** — The red and green centriole channels may exhibit spectral bleed‑through, complicating assignment of mother vs. daughter centriole identity.
4. **Generalisation** — The fine‑tuned model is validated on BJ‑hTERT cells only; performance on other cell lines or imaging conditions is not yet tested.
5. **Spotiflow reliability for moving centrioles** — Spotiflow (Dohmen et al. 2024) provides sub‑pixel localization in fixed or slow‑moving samples, but its performance on live, motile centrioles (e.g., during prometaphase oscillation, anaphase migration) has not been extensively benchmarked. Motion blur and out‑of‑focus frames may degrade detection accuracy. To address this, we will (a) validate spotiflow on a manually tracked set of ≥200 centriole trajectories using metrics such as root‑mean‑square deviation (RMSD) and tracking precision (Jaqaman et al. 2008, Nat Methods; PMID: 18657544), (b) incorporate temporal context via a Kalman filter to recover missed detections, and (c) pre‑register a sensitivity analysis excluding frames with estimated motion >2 pixels per frame (which correspond to the fastest anaphase movements). If spotiflow performance is inadequate (centriole F1 <0.85), we will fall back on a custom CNN regression model trained on our own data.

These limitations affect the reliability of centriole tracking in a fraction of frames and will be mitigated by manual curation of ambiguous cases and by incorporating temporal context (tracking across frames). Additionally, if tracking fails we will fall back on TrackMate for cell‑level tracking and use manual annotation for centrioles in difficult cases.
