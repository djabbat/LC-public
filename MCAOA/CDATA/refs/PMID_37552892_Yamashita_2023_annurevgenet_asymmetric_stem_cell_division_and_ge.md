Germ cells are the only cell type that is capable of transmitting genetic 
information to the next generation, which has enabled the continuation of 
multicellular life for the last 1.5 billion years. Surprisingly little is known 
about the mechanisms supporting the germline's remarkable ability to continue in 
this eternal cycle, termed germline immortality. Even unicellular organisms age 
at a cellular level, demonstrating that cellular aging is inevitable. Extensive 
studies in yeast have established the framework of how asymmetric cell division 
and gametogenesis may contribute to the resetting of cellular age. This review 
examines the mechanisms of germline immortality-how germline cells reset the 
aging of cells-drawing a parallel between yeast and multicellular organisms.
