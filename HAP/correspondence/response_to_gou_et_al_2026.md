## To the Editor:

I read with great interest the article by Gou et al. (2026), "Beyond the 'second brain': the gut microbiota as a constitutive co-constructor of embodied cognitive network" (Front. Neurosci. 20:1808839). The authors present a carefully argued case for the gut microbiota as a constitutive contributor to interoceptive, affective, and vagal-metabolic architectures of cognition. I commend their systematic evidential chain — deprivation, replacement, observation, intervention — and their nuanced use of the mutual manipulability criterion to distinguish constitutive relevance from mere causal influence.

However, I wish to raise a fundamental concern: the authors' framework, while sophisticated in its treatment of the microbiome, **entirely neglects the macrobiome** — the social, linguistic, and cultural environment into which every human is born and without which no human cognitive architecture can develop.

More than neglect, this represents a category error: the microbiome and the macrobiome are not independent layers of cognition but **competing forces** that vie for control of the organism's predictive apparatus. The microbiome presses from within — through interoceptive, metabolic, and immune channels. The macrobiome presses from without — through language, social scripts, and cultural norms. The cognitive functions we observe are the *product of their competition*, not the sum of their separate contributions. By analysing only the microbiome, the authors describe one competitor while mistaking it for the entire game.

## The macrobiome: competitor, not bystander

A child raised in complete isolation from human language — deprived of the macrobiome — will **never learn to speak**, regardless of the integrity of their gut microbiome. Conversely, a germ-free animal colonized with human microbiota does not spontaneously acquire syntactic competence, abstract reasoning, or cultural literacy. The macrobiome — parents, teachers, peers, language, writing systems, scientific traditions — is the environment that teaches a human being to think, speak, and reason. **No microbe has ever taught a child to form a grammatically correct sentence.**

The point is not merely that the macrobiome is important — it is that the macrobiome and the microbiome are **competitors for the same predictive architecture**. The organism builds a world model to anticipate events and guide action. Both the macrobiome and the microbiome seek to shape that model. During wakefulness, the macrobiome dominates — language, task demands, and social context restructure the predictive apparatus. During sleep, the microbiome gains ascendancy — recalibrating interoceptive baselines and modifying the predictive models that wakefulness left unexplored. Cognition is the interference pattern produced by their ongoing competition.

The authors' five criteria for constitutiveness apply to the macrobiome **at least as strongly** as to the microbiome:

1. **Spatial location**: the macrobiome is not inside the body, but the brain's predictive processing architecture is fundamentally shaped by it — the environment is part of the cognitive loop (Clark and Chalmers, 1998).

2. **Temporal continuity**: language and culture precede the individual and persist after them — they are the most temporally continuous feature of human cognition.

3. **Functional integration**: language is woven into every cognitive process — planning, memory, reasoning, emotion regulation. Disrupt language (e.g., aphasia), and you disrupt cognition far more profoundly than disrupting the microbiome.

4. **Participation in cognitive architecture**: the macrobiome does not merely modulate — it **constitutes** the symbolic and computational infrastructure of human cognition.

5. **Developmental embedding**: a child deprived of language before age ~7 will never acquire full linguistic competence (critical period hypothesis). A child deprived of a normal microbiome will have metabolic and immune alterations but will still learn to speak.

If we apply the authors' own logic, the macrobiome is a constitutive co-constructor — but more importantly, it is a **competitor** to the microbiome for control of the predictive apparatus. The authors' selective focus on the microbial dimension while ignoring the macrobial one represents a category error: **describing one player while mistaking it for the entire game**.

## The digital twin opportunity — and why the authors should embrace it

Here I come to a constructive proposal. The authors explicitly mention, in their Section 5 (Future Perspectives), the importance of "computational models and digital twins" — specifically, "building personalized virtual microbiota-brain models and simulating long-term interventions *in silico* to provide predictive blueprints for individualized clinical strategies."

I could not agree more — but this vision is incomplete without **physical simulation**.

At the Georgia Longevity Alliance, we are developing precisely such a system: a **whole-body physical simulator** — a comprehensive *in vitro* platform that replicates the human organism's key physiological systems, including:

- **The oral cavity** — with its distinct microbial ecology
- **The urogenital system** — with its unique microbiome
- **The small intestine** — with its nutrient absorption, peristalsis, and microbial communities
- **The large intestine** — with its dense fermentative microbiota, SCFA production, and vagal signaling interfaces

Our vision is a **tightly coupled digital-physical twin**: a computational model that learns from and guides the physical simulator, while the physical simulator provides real metabolomic, immunologic, and electrophysiological data that no purely *in silico* model can currently generate.

This platform would allow researchers to:
- Test causal hypotheses about microbiota-host interactions in a controlled, reproducible system
- Measure real-time metabolite fluxes (SCFAs, bile acids, indoles, GABA precursors) across multiple gut compartments
- Manipulate specific microbial consortia and observe downstream effects on simulated neural signaling
- Validate digital twin predictions against physical measurements in a closed loop

The authors of Gou et al. would be ideal collaborators for this effort. Their theoretical framework — the microbiota as a co-constructor — could be rigorously tested and refined using our combined digital-physical simulation platform. Conversely, our platform would provide the kind of **mechanistic evidence** the authors acknowledge is still missing (Section 4.2, limitations): the ability to trace the full causal chain from "strain/metabolite → cell-specific receptor → neural circuit dynamics → behavioral output."

## A call for collaboration

I therefore respectfully ask the authors to initiate a discussion about a joint research collaboration. Specifically:

1. **Joint development** of a digital-physical twin of the human gut-brain axis, integrating the authors' computational models with our physical simulator platform.

2. **Cross-validation** of the constitutive hypothesis: our physical simulator can test predictions about microbial necessity, sufficiency, and online participation in cognitive signaling under conditions that are impossible to achieve *in vivo*.

3. **Translation to clinical applications**: a validated digital-physical twin would accelerate the development of precision psychobiotics, targeted metabolic mimetics, and personalized microbiome interventions — exactly the translational goals the authors outline.

The authors are right that the gut microbiota is a deeply integrated biological dimension of cognition. But cognition emerges from the *competition* between the microbiome and the macrobiome for the organism's predictive apparatus — not from either force alone. A complete framework must describe the dynamics of this competition. Our digital-physical simulation platform offers a concrete path toward that description.

I look forward to a productive discussion and, I hope, collaboration.
