## A theoretical concern: the missing macrobiome

Your framework identifies the gut microbiota as a constitutive co-constructor of cognition. I find your five criteria — spatial location, temporal continuity, functional integration, participation in cognitive architecture, and developmental embedding — to be well-chosen and rigorous.

However, I would suggest that these criteria, applied systematically, point to another essential force: what I term the **macrobiome** — the social, linguistic, and cultural environment into which every human infant is born. A child deprived of language during the critical period will never acquire full linguistic or abstract reasoning abilities, regardless of microbiome integrity. The microbiome and macrobiome are not separate domains of cognition but forces that compete for the organism's predictive apparatus — and cognition emerges from their ongoing interaction.

This is not to diminish the importance of the microbial dimension — far from it. Rather, I believe a truly comprehensive framework must describe how these two forces jointly shape the predictive architecture of cognition. I have attached a more detailed commentary for your consideration.

## A proposal for collaboration

The reason I am writing, however, is primarily constructive. In Section 5 of your article, you discuss the need for "computational models and digital twins" to validate the constitutive hypothesis. I could not agree more — and I believe we have something unique to offer.

At the **Georgia Longevity Alliance** (Tbilisi, Georgia), we are developing a **whole-body physical simulator** — a comprehensive *in vitro* platform that replicates the key compartments of the human organism:

- **Oral cavity** — with its distinct microbial ecology
- **Urogenital system** — with its unique microbiome
- **Small intestine** — with nutrient absorption, peristalsis, and microbial communities
- **Large intestine** — with dense fermentative microbiota, SCFA production, and vagal signaling interfaces

The platform is conceived as a **tightly coupled digital-physical twin**: a computational model that learns from and guides the physical simulator, while the simulator provides real metabolomic, immunologic, and electrophysiological data that no purely *in silico* model can currently generate.

Specifically, our platform would allow us to:

1. **Test causal hypotheses** about microbiota-host interactions under controlled, reproducible conditions — exactly the kind of mechanistic evidence your framework requires
2. **Measure real-time metabolite fluxes** (SCFAs, bile acids, indoles, GABA precursors) across multiple gut compartments
3. **Manipulate specific microbial consortia** and observe downstream effects on simulated neural signaling
4. **Trace the full causal chain** from strain/metabolite → cell-specific receptor → neural circuit dynamics → behavioural output — precisely the gap you identify in Section 4.2
5. **Validate digital twin predictions** against physical measurements in a closed loop

## What we propose

We would like to explore a joint collaboration around three goals:

**1. Joint development** of a digital-physical twin of the human gut-brain axis, integrating your computational models with our physical simulator platform.

**2. Cross-validation** of the constitutive hypothesis: our physical simulator can test predictions about microbial necessity, sufficiency, and online participation in cognitive signaling under conditions that are impossible *in vivo*.

**3. Translation to clinical applications**: a validated digital-physical twin would accelerate the development of precision psychobiotics, targeted metabolic mimetics, and personalized microbiome interventions — exactly the translational goals you outline.
