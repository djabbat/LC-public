The central idea

Every dividing cell inherits two centrioles. They are not equivalent: one is the mother, several cell-cycles old, decorated with sub-distal appendages and accumulated post-translational modifications; the other is the daughter, just synthesised. In adult stem cell divisions the inheritance is asymmetric — the older mother centriole stays in the cell that retains stem-cell identity; the newer one goes with the differentiating daughter.

This means the stem cell lineage carries, generation after generation, the same physical centriole — and any damage it accumulates. Polyglutamylation, a tubulin post-translational modification that increases monotonically with centriole age, is the proposed read-out of this damage. CDATA is the claim that cellular aging in the lineage is, to a substantial extent, the aging of that one inherited mother centriole.

Why asymmetric inheritance matters

If centrioles were symmetrically inherited (a fresh stem cell each division), no damage could accumulate across generations — every daughter would start from a half-aged template. Asymmetric inheritance violates this reset, and it does so in exactly the cell type — adult stem cells — where lifetime damage accumulation has the largest organism-level consequence.

Yamashita's work on Drosophila germline stem cells (Yamashita 2007, Science) was the first direct evidence of biased mother-centriole retention. Since then, similar bias has been observed in mouse neural stem cells (Wang 2009) and human radial glia (Paridaen 2013). The pattern is not universal — some lineages are symmetric, some are biased toward the daughter — but where bias toward the mother exists, CDATA predicts disproportionate aging in that lineage.

Molecular mechanism

Polyglutamylation as a damage clock

Tubulin polyglutamylation is added by TTLL family glutamylases and removed by CCP family glutamate carboxypeptidases. On centrioles the steady-state balance favours addition, so polyglutamylation accumulates monotonically with centriole age. Long polyglutamate chains alter centriole stability and cilium-assembly fidelity (Lacroix 2010, J Cell Biol).

Cilium/centrosome dual-use

The mother centriole is also the basal body of the primary cilium — the organelle that transduces Hedgehog and Wnt signals essential to stem-cell identity. Polyglutamylation-induced cilium dysfunction is therefore a plausible link from "centriole age" to "stem-cell signalling failure".

From a single centriole to organism-level effect

Each tissue has 10⁵–10⁹ stem cells, each with its own asymmetrically inherited mother centriole. The CDATA claim is statistical: on average across this population, polyglutamylation rises with chronological time, and this rise correlates with stem-cell exhaustion phenotypes. Single-cell heterogeneity is allowed and predicted.

Counter equation (within MCAOA framework)

CDATA is registered as a candidate Counter in the MCAOA framework with the following damage-accumulation equation:

D_centriole(n, t) = D_centriole,0 + α₂·(n/n₂*) + β₂·(t/τ₂) + γ₂·I(others)

where α₂ is division-linked (each division stages the mother for further glutamylation), β₂ is time-linked (slow basal accumulation in quiescent cells), and γ₂ couples to other counters — most importantly mitochondrial ROS, which oxidises tubulin and accelerates polyglutamylation.

The candidate kinetic regime is α-dominated: division events drive damage accumulation more strongly than the passage of chronological time. This is testable by comparing high-turnover (intestinal crypt) vs. low-turnover (cortical neuron) tissue trajectories in BioSense χ_Ze data.

Falsifiable predictions — E0 experimental programme

CDATA is tested through the E0 experimental rig (Kaede photoconversion + selective ablation, ~/Desktop/PhD/E0/), and through downstream cell-biological measurements — not through Ze/χ_Ze biomarker readings, which belong to a separate project. Each prediction below states the experimental venue (E0 → in-vitro lineage tracking → in-vivo HSC) and the quantitative threshold.

FP-B1 — Centrosome amplification correlates with replicative age

Venue: E0 lineage-tracking on Kaede-tagged HSCs, paired with centrosome amplification index (CAI) from immunofluorescence. Prediction: within a single Kaede-marked clone, Spearman ρ(division count, CAI) > 0.6 across N ≥ 200 single-cell measurements (p < 0.01). Falsified if ρ ≤ 0.3 at α = 0.01.

FP-B2 — ROCKi reduces centriolar damage rate

Venue: in-vitro mouse HSC culture with Y-27632 (10 µM) for 30 days, paired vehicle control, polyglutamylation read-out via GT335 immunostaining. Prediction: ROCKi-treated cohort shows ≥ 30 % reduction in mean polyglutamylation intensity per inherited mother centriole at matched division count. Falsified if Δ ≤ 10 % at α = 0.01.

FP-B3 — Mother-centriole retention ↔ stem-cell exhaustion

Venue: E0 selective ablation — photo-ablate cells inheriting the old mother centriole (Kaede-red) and compare functional outcomes to ablation of new-centriole inheritors (Kaede-green) in the same clone. Prediction: the lineage retaining the old mother shows accelerated exhaustion phenotype (CFU-S decline, loss of self-renewal markers) vs. the new-centriole control. Falsified if no phenotypic divergence at 60 d post-ablation.

FP-B4 — Hayflick saturation of polyglutamylation

Venue: serial passage of human MSCs to the Hayflick limit, polyglutamylation read-out per passage. Prediction: polyglutamylation rises monotonically and saturates near the Hayflick limit (D_centriole → 1). The saturation point and shape define the f(D) curve in the MCAOA equation. Falsified if the curve is non-monotonic or fails to saturate.

FP-B5 — α dominance over β

Venue: high-turnover (intestinal crypt, ν ≈ 1/day) vs. low-turnover (cortical neuron, ν ≈ 0/year) tissues at matched chronological age. Prediction: at matched age the high-turnover tissue shows greater D_centriole accumulation. Falsified if β > α (time-dominance), which would collapse CDATA to a generic time-clock and remove its distinctive prediction.

E0 — the experimental rig that tests CDATA

What E0 is

E0 is a custom Kaede photoconversion + selective-ablation microscopy rig built specifically to discriminate the role of the inherited mother centriole. Kaede tagging marks the cell at a known division event; subsequent divisions inherit the tag asymmetrically, allowing tracking of which daughter retained the old mother centriole. Targeted photo-ablation then removes the chosen population and measures the consequence on lineage fitness.

Phase 1 — bench validation (in progress)

BOM: ~$106 from AliExpress + 300 GEL local fabrication. Lead: Giorgi Tsomaia (Tbilisi). Mentee: Ilia Zheleznov (HSC simulator integration). The rig validates the optical path and ablation geometry on cultured MSCs before HSC work begins.

Phase 2 — HSC integration

Once the rig is bench-validated, the same protocol is run on murine HSCs in collaboration with the Geiger lab (Ulm) — see Phase B below.

FAQ

Is CDATA the same as the centrosome theory of aging?

No. The "centrosome theory" historically refers to errors in mitotic spindle organisation as a cause of aneuploidy and senescence — a structural/segregation argument. CDATA is a kinetic theory about damage accumulation on a specific physical organelle inherited asymmetrically across divisions; the polyglutamylation read-out is independent of segregation errors.

What if asymmetric inheritance turns out to be tissue-specific or species-specific?

That would narrow CDATA's scope, not falsify the framework. CDATA is registered in MCAOA as a candidate counter where asymmetric inheritance is documented; in tissues with symmetric inheritance, the predicted weight w_centriole(tissue) would be near zero. FP-B5 (α dominance over β) is the actual falsification anchor — if division rate doesn't drive damage, CDATA fails everywhere.

How does CDATA interact with other counters?

The γ₂ coupling term explicitly allows interaction. Predicted: mitochondrial ROS ↑ → tubulin oxidation ↑ → polyglutamylation rate ↑. This is testable via ROS-quenching interventions (mitoQ) and should produce a measurable Δα₂.
